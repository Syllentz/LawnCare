Bermuda Grass at a Glance

Healthy Bermuda grass (Cynodon dactylon) — dense, low-growing, and thrives in full sun

Why Bermuda Is Different

Bermuda is a warm-season grass that thrives in heat and goes dormant (turns brown) in winter. In Cypress (Zone 9a), the growing season is extra long — roughly late March through late November. This means:

  • You fertilize April through early November in Cypress
  • You mow it much shorter — 1 to 2 inches
  • You do a spring scalp to kickstart green-up
  • You stop nitrogen 6 weeks before first frost (early November here)

Bermuda Quick Stats

  • Mowing height: 1–2" (rotary mower) or 0.5–1" (reel mower)
  • Mowing frequency: Every 5–7 days during peak growth
  • Fertilizer needs: 2–4 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft per year
  • Water needs: 1–1.25" per week
  • Sun requirements: Full sun (6–8+ hours daily)
  • Active growth: When soil temps are above 65°F
  • Goes dormant: Below 55°F soil temp (turns brown — this is normal)

Mowing Height & the Spring Scalp

Yes, Cut It Short! Bermuda grass wants to be mowed low. Unlike fescue or bluegrass (which prefer 3–4"), Bermuda performs best at 1–2 inches with a rotary mower. If you have a reel mower, you can maintain it as low as 0.5–1". Lower heights produce a denser, more carpet-like lawn.

The Spring Scalp (Critical!)

The spring scalp is one of the most important things you can do for Bermuda grass. It removes dead, brown dormant growth and lets sunlight reach the soil to warm it up faster.

  • When: After the last hard freeze but before new green blades emerge (typically late March in Cypress — the last freeze is usually mid-February)
  • How low: Scalp to roughly 50% of your target height. If you normally mow at 1.5", scalp down to 0.75". If you normally mow at 2", scalp to 1".
  • Bag the clippings during the scalp — remove all the dead material
  • Your lawn will look rough for 2–3 weeks, then the green will explode
  • This is the ONE time you intentionally break the 1/3 rule

The One-Third Rule (Rest of Year)

Outside of the spring scalp, never remove more than 1/3 of the grass blade in a single mowing. Because Bermuda is kept low, this means:

  • At 1.5" height: mow when it reaches ~2.25"
  • At 1" height: mow when it reaches ~1.5"
  • In peak summer, this means mowing every 4–5 days
  • Bermuda grows aggressively — if you skip mowing, you'll have to gradually lower the height over multiple sessions to avoid scalping
Rotary vs. Reel Mower: A rotary mower (standard push/riding mower) works fine for Bermuda at 1.5–2". But if you want that ultra-low, golf-course look below 1", you'll need a reel mower. Rotary mowers can't cut cleanly that low and will scalp unevenly.

Mowing Best Practices

Mowing Frequency

  • Spring green-up (late March–April): Every 7–10 days as growth starts
  • Peak growth (May–Sep): Every 4–7 days
  • Slowing down (Oct–Nov): Every 7–10 days
  • Dormancy (mid-Dec–late Feb): No mowing needed

Bermuda grows fast and lateral. Frequent mowing encourages it to spread via stolons and rhizomes, filling in bare spots and creating a denser lawn.

Best Time of Day to Mow

  • Ideal: Mid-morning (8–10 AM) after dew dries
  • Good: Late afternoon (4–6 PM) as heat fades
  • Avoid: Midday heat — stresses freshly cut grass
  • Never: When grass is wet from rain or dew

Alternate Your Pattern

Switch mowing direction every time — horizontal, vertical, diagonal. This prevents ruts, soil compaction, and grass grain (leaning one direction). It also gives a cleaner, more professional look.

Mulch the Clippings

Leave clippings on the lawn. They return nitrogen to the soil and can supply up to 25% of your fertilizer needs. Only bag clippings during the spring scalp or if they're clumping heavily.

Blade Sharpness: Sharpen mower blades every 20–25 hours of use (roughly every 2–3 weeks during peak mowing). Dull blades shred Bermuda instead of cutting it, leaving white/brown tips and inviting disease.

  • Begin watering routine: If rain is scarce, provide 1–1.25" per week in 2–3 deep sessions.

  • Fertilize: Apply 0.5–1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft. Bermuda is a heavy feeder and loves summer nitrogen.
  • Grub prevention: Apply preventive grub control (chlorantraniliprole-based products) mid-June.
  • Water: 1–1.25" per week. Water early morning (before 10 AM).

  • Mowing: Continue every 4–5 days at 1–2". Bermuda thrives in summer heat — this is when it looks its best.
  • Fertilize (July 4th): Apply 0.5–1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft. Milorganite is a great organic option — it won't burn in the heat.
  • Potassium boost: If your soil test showed low K, apply 0.5 lb potash (K2O) per 1,000 sq ft to build heat and drought tolerance.
  • Water deeply if rainfall is insufficient. Bermuda is drought-tolerant but performs best with consistent moisture.

  • Fertilize: Apply 0.5–1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft. In Cypress, this is NOT your last feeding — with a mid-December first frost, you can continue feeding through October.

  • Spot-treat weeds as needed.

  • Fertilize: You can still apply 0.5 lb N per 1,000 sq ft. Bermuda is far from dormancy in the Houston area — the N cutoff isn't until early November.
  • Potassium application (optional): Apply 1 lb potash (K2O) per 1,000 sq ft to boost winter hardiness. In Cypress, this can also be pushed to October.
  • Fall pre-emergent: Apply when soil temps drop to 70°F, which in Cypress is typically mid-to-late October. If temps are still high in September, wait.
  • Core aeration (if needed): Late spring/early summer is ideal for Bermuda, but September still works — the grass is still actively growing and will recover quickly in this climate.
OCTOBER Growth Begins to Slow
  • Mowing: Bermuda is still green and growing in Cypress through October. Continue mowing every 7–10 days as growth starts to slow.
  • Light fertilizer (early Oct): You can still apply a light feeding — 0.5 lb N per 1,000 sq ft. The N cutoff for Cypress is early November.
  • Fall pre-emergent: Apply in mid-to-late October when soil temps drop to 70°F to prevent Poa annua and winter annual weeds.
  • Potassium: If you haven't applied potash yet, now is the time — 1 lb K2O per 1,000 sq ft for winter hardiness.
  • Leaf management: Mulch or remove fallen leaves. A thick leaf layer smothers Bermuda and traps moisture, encouraging disease.
NOVEMBER Last N Feeding & Wind-Down
  • Last nitrogen (early November): Apply a final light feeding of 0.5 lb N per 1,000 sq ft in early November. This is your N cutoff — 6 weeks before Cypress's mid-December first frost.
  • Bermuda starts browning: In Cypress, Bermuda typically starts turning brown in late November or early December. It stays green longer here than in most areas.
  • Last mow (late Nov – early Dec): Give the lawn a final cut at your normal height (1–2") once growth stops. In mild years, you may still be mowing into early December.
  • Clean up leaves: Don't let leaves sit on Bermuda as it goes dormant.
  • Winterize your mower: Run fuel stabilizer, clean the deck, sharpen blades for next spring.
MID-DECEMBER – LATE FEBRUARY Winter Dormancy (Shorter in Cypress)
  • Dormancy is shorter here: In Cypress, Bermuda often stays semi-green well into December in mild years. True dormancy is roughly mid-December through late February — shorter than most Bermuda regions.
  • Stay off frozen grass — hard freezes are less common in Cypress but do happen. Foot traffic on frozen dormant Bermuda can damage crowns.
  • Plan ahead: Review soil test results, order pre-emergent and fertilizer for spring. Remember: pre-emergent can be needed as early as late February here.
  • Optional: Some homeowners overseed dormant Bermuda with perennial ryegrass for winter green color. This is purely cosmetic and can slightly delay Bermuda's spring green-up.

Fertilizing Your Bermuda Lawn

Bermuda is a hungry grass. It needs 2–4 lbs of actual nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft per year, split across 5–6 applications during the growing season (April–early November in Cypress). The extended growing season in Zone 9a means you can spread feedings out more and keep your lawn green longer.

Understanding NPK Numbers

Every fertilizer bag shows three numbers (e.g., 32-0-10). These tell you the percentage by weight:

  • N (Nitrogen) — 1st number: Drives green color and blade growth. Bermuda's #1 nutrient.
  • P (Phosphorus) — 2nd number: Root and seedling development. Usually not needed unless a soil test says otherwise — many soils already have plenty.
  • K (Potassium) — 3rd number: Stress tolerance, disease resistance, and cold hardiness. Critical for Bermuda going into winter.

How to Calculate "Actual Nitrogen"

When we say "apply 1 lb of nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft," we mean actual N, not 1 lb of product. Here's the math:

Formula: Pounds of product = Desired N ÷ (N% on bag ÷ 100)

Example with a 32-0-10 fertilizer:
To apply 1 lb of actual N:   1 ÷ 0.32 = 3.125 lbs of product per 1,000 sq ft

Example with Milorganite 6-4-0:
To apply 1 lb of actual N:   1 ÷ 0.06 = 16.7 lbs of product per 1,000 sq ft (about 1/3 of a standard 32 lb bag)

Always check the bag label — many products list the spreader setting and coverage area for you.

Slow-Release vs. Quick-Release Nitrogen

Slow-Release (Recommended)

  • Feeds grass gradually over 6–10 weeks
  • Lower burn risk, even in heat
  • More even growth (less mowing surge)
  • Look for: "slow-release," "controlled-release," "coated urea," or "sulfur-coated"
  • Examples: Milorganite, Scotts Turf Builder

Quick-Release

  • Fast green-up within days
  • Higher burn risk if over-applied or in heat
  • Causes a surge of growth (more mowing)
  • Best used sparingly or in cooler temps
  • Examples: urea (46-0-0), ammonium sulfate (21-0-0)

How to Apply Fertilizer

  • Use a broadcast (rotary) spreader for even coverage. Drop spreaders work too but leave visible stripes if you overlap poorly.
  • Apply in two passes at half rate: one pass north-south, one east-west. This ensures even coverage and prevents striping.
  • Water in after applying — give the lawn 0.25–0.5" of water within 24 hours to wash granules off the blades and into the soil. Fertilizer sitting on wet grass blades causes burns.
  • Apply on dry grass — granules stick to wet blades and burn them. Apply when the grass is dry, then water in afterward.
  • Don't fertilize before heavy rain — a downpour washes product into storm drains before the soil can absorb it.
  • Clean up spills — sweep any fertilizer off sidewalks and driveways back onto the lawn. Concentrated piles will burn.
  • Calibrate your spreader — check the bag for recommended spreader settings. Do a test run on a driveway to see the spread pattern before hitting the lawn.

Soil Testing — Don't Skip This

A $15–20 soil test from your local county extension office tells you exactly what your soil needs. Without one, you're guessing. It reveals:

  • pH level: Bermuda prefers 6.0–7.0. If soil is too acidic, add lime. Too alkaline, add sulfur.
  • Phosphorus & Potassium levels: You may not need P at all, saving money and preventing runoff.
  • Micronutrient deficiencies: Iron deficiency is common in Bermuda and causes yellowing even when N is sufficient. An iron supplement (like Ironite or ferrous sulfate) gives a quick dark green boost without extra growth.

Test in early spring or late summer. Results take 1–2 weeks. Most extension offices provide specific fertilizer recommendations with the results.

Iron for Color Without Growth: Want your Bermuda darker green without pushing extra growth? Apply ferrous sulfate or a chelated iron product. It deepens color within days without adding nitrogen. Great for a quick visual boost between fertilizer applications. Apply at 2 oz per 1,000 sq ft mixed in water, or use a granular product like Ironite.

Watering Guide

How Much

  • Bermuda needs 1 – 1.25 inches of water per week (including rainfall)
  • Apply in 2–3 deep sessions, not daily light sprinkles
  • Deep, infrequent watering trains roots to grow deep
  • Bermuda is drought-tolerant — it will go dormant (brown) in extreme drought but bounces back when watered
  • Place a tuna can on the lawn to measure sprinkler output

When & How

  • Best time: Early morning (5–10 AM) before heat causes evaporation
  • Avoid: Evening watering — grass stays wet overnight, promoting fungal disease (Bermuda is susceptible to brown patch and dollar spot)
  • Signs of thirst: Bermuda folds its leaf blades inward, turns a blue-gray color, and footprints linger instead of bouncing back
  • Sandy soil: shorter, more frequent watering
  • Clay soil: slower application to prevent runoff

Aeration & Dethatching

Bermuda Builds Thatch Fast. Because Bermuda grows aggressively via stolons and rhizomes, it accumulates thatch faster than most grasses. If thatch exceeds 0.5 inches, water and nutrients can't reach the soil. Dethatch and aerate regularly.

Core Aeration

  • Best time: Late spring to early summer (May–June) when Bermuda is actively growing and can recover quickly
  • Use a core aerator (pulls plugs), not a spike aerator
  • Make 2–3 passes in different directions
  • Soil should be moist, not saturated
  • Leave plugs on the surface to break down
  • Aerate annually for heavy clay, every 2 years for sandy soil

Dethatching

  • Best time: Late spring (May) after Bermuda is fully green and growing vigorously
  • Dethatch when the thatch layer exceeds 0.5 inches
  • Use a power dethatcher (vertical mower) set to cut into the thatch but not the soil
  • Bermuda recovers aggressively — it will fill in bare spots within 2–4 weeks
  • Never dethatch dormant Bermuda — it can't recover
  • Ideal sequence: dethatch → aerate → fertilize

What About Raking?

Raking is a different question from dethatching, and for Bermuda lawns it's generally not recommended as a routine practice. Here's the breakdown:

When You SHOULD Rake
  • After the spring scalp — rake or bag up the dead material you just cut so sunlight reaches the soil
  • Leaf removal in fall — a thick layer of leaves smothers dormant Bermuda and traps moisture, leading to fungal disease
  • Debris cleanup — sticks, pine straw, or storm debris sitting on the lawn
  • Before overseeding — if you winter-overseed with ryegrass, raking first improves seed-to-soil contact
When You Should NOT Rake
  • Regular grass clippings — leave them! Mulched clippings return nitrogen to the soil (up to 25% of fertilizer needs) and break down fast
  • During active growth — aggressive raking can rip up Bermuda's shallow stolons and damage the turf
  • As a substitute for dethatching — a hand rake can't remove true thatch (the spongy layer below the blades). You need a power dethatcher for that.
  • Frozen/dormant lawn — raking dormant Bermuda in winter can tear crowns and thin the lawn come spring
Bottom line: For Bermuda, raking is a situational cleanup tool (spring scalp debris, fall leaves), not a regular maintenance step. During the growing season, your mower's mulching function handles clippings, and a power dethatcher handles thatch. Raking for the sake of raking just risks damaging the stolons that make Bermuda spread and fill in.

Lawn Problem Diagnosis & Fixes

See something wrong? Find the symptom below and follow the fix.

Weed Problems

Crabgrass — low-growing, light green, spreading clumps

Crabgrass spreading along the ground

Crabgrass plant — note the star-like spreading stems

Prevent It (Best Approach):

  • Apply pre-emergent in late February–early March in Cypress when soil hits 55°F
  • Products: Prodiamine 65 WDG, Scotts Halts, or Dimension (dithiopyr)
  • A second application 6–8 weeks later extends protection

Kill Existing Crabgrass:

  • Quinclorac (Drive XLR8) — safe on Bermuda, kills crabgrass post-emergent
  • Apply when crabgrass is young (2–4 tillers). Large mature plants are harder to kill.
  • Add a surfactant for better adhesion

Clover — low, round 3-leaf clusters with white flowers

White clover — 3-leaf clusters with white puffball flowers

Closeup of clover flower head

Why It's There:

  • Clover thrives in low-nitrogen soils. It's a sign your lawn needs more fertilizer.
  • It also appears in thin, patchy areas where Bermuda isn't dense enough to crowd it out.

How to Fix:

  • Increase nitrogen — a well-fed Bermuda lawn chokes out clover naturally
  • Spot-spray with Celsius WG or any triclopyr-based herbicide (safe on Bermuda)
  • Avoid 2,4-D + dicamba combos in summer heat — they can stress Bermuda above 85°F

Dandelions — yellow flowers, jagged leaves, deep taproot

Dandelions scattered across a lawn

Fix: Spot-spray with Celsius WG, 2,4-D broadleaf killer, or Spectracide Weed Stop when temps are 60–85°F and dandelions are actively growing. They pull easily by hand too — use a dandelion puller tool to get the whole taproot. Preventing them: a thick, well-fed Bermuda lawn leaves no room for dandelion seeds to establish.

Poa Annua (Annual Bluegrass) — light green patches, seed heads in spring

Poa annua — lighter green clumps with white seed heads

Fix: Poa annua is Bermuda's #1 winter weed enemy. It germinates in fall when Bermuda goes dormant. Prevention is the only real solution: apply a fall pre-emergent (Prodiamine or Barricade) in mid-to-late October in Cypress when soil temps drop to 70°F. Once it's established, there's no selective herbicide that kills it without harming Bermuda. The good news: it dies on its own in summer heat.

Nutsedge — bright yellow-green, grows faster than grass, triangular stem

Yellow nutsedge in a lawn — note the bright green color vs surrounding grass

Fix: Nutsedge is NOT a grass — it's a sedge, so regular herbicides don't work. Use SedgeHammer (halosulfuron) or Dismiss (sulfentrazone). Both are safe on Bermuda. Apply when nutsedge is actively growing (summer). It spreads by underground tubers (nutlets), so persistence over 2–3 seasons may be needed to fully eliminate it.

Lawn Color & Appearance Issues

Brown Patches — circular dead-looking areas

Brown patch fungus — circular dead patch with darker ring at edge

Dollar spot — small silver-dollar-sized tan spots

Possible Causes:

  • Brown Patch fungus — circular patches with dark outer ring, common in humid weather with night temps above 68°F
  • Dollar Spot — small silver-dollar-sized tan spots, common when nitrogen is low
  • Grub damage — turf pulls up like a carpet (grubs eat roots). Worst in late summer/fall.
    White grub (Japanese beetle larva) — C-shaped, found just below soil surface
  • Scalping — cutting too low in one spot (uneven ground or dull blades)

Fixes:

  • Fungus: Apply a fungicide containing azoxystrobin (Heritage, Scott's DiseaseEx) or propiconazole. Reduce evening watering.
  • Dollar Spot: Increase nitrogen feeding — it often resolves on its own with proper fertilization.
  • Grubs: Apply GrubEx (chlorantraniliprole) preventively in June, or Dylox (trichlorfon) as a curative if grubs are already present. Pull up a section of turf — more than 5 grubs per sq ft = treatment needed.
  • Scalping: Level low spots with sand topdressing over time. Raise your mow height slightly.

Entire Lawn Turning Yellow — general yellowing, not patchy

Possible Causes:

  • Iron deficiency — very common in Bermuda, especially in alkaline soils (pH above 7)
  • Nitrogen deficiency — hasn't been fed recently
  • Overwatering — roots are drowning, can't uptake nutrients
  • Compacted soil — roots can't expand

Fixes:

  • Iron: Apply ferrous sulfate (2 oz per 1,000 sq ft in water) or Ironite. Green-up in 2–3 days.
  • Nitrogen: Apply a balanced fertilizer per the schedule above.
  • Overwatering: Cut back to 1–1.25" per week. Let the top inch of soil dry between waterings.
  • Compaction: Core aerate in late spring.

Thin / Patchy Bermuda — bare spots, not filling in

Causes & Fixes:

  • Not enough sun: Bermuda needs 6–8+ hours of direct sun. Shaded areas will always be thin. Consider replacing deep shade spots with a shade-tolerant ground cover or mulch.
  • Soil compaction: Core aerate and topdress with sand. Bermuda stolons can't spread through brick-hard soil.
  • Under-fertilized: Bermuda needs 2–4 lbs N/year. If you're under-feeding, it won't spread aggressively.
  • Mowing too infrequently: Frequent low mowing stimulates lateral growth. If you only mow every 2 weeks, Bermuda grows tall instead of spreading.
  • For bare spots: Bermuda fills in on its own if conditions are right (sun, water, fertilizer, low mow). For faster results, apply Bermuda seed or plugs in late spring/early summer when soil temps are above 65°F.

White / Ragged Blade Tips — shredded-looking grass tips

Clean, straight cut
Heals quickly, stays green
vs
Dull Blade

Cause: Dull mower blades. Instead of a clean cut, they're tearing the grass. The ragged edges lose moisture, turn white, then brown — making the whole lawn look hazy or washed out from a distance.

Fix: Sharpen your blades. This is the single easiest improvement you can make. Sharpen every 20–25 hours of use, or swap to a fresh blade mid-season. A sharp blade makes an immediate visible difference.
